Getting Started

Account, Plans & Upgrading

Signing in is free and gets your data synced across every device — your saved layouts, tool tracking, Project Designer work, Shop Showcase posts, and Tool Reviews all live on your account, not just in one browser.

  1. Click Sign In in the top-right corner of any page.
  2. Click New here? Create an account, enter an email and password, and submit.
  3. Check your email to confirm the account, then sign back in.
  4. Click your new account button (top-right) to set a username and avatar.

Plans

Kerph has three tiers — Free, Pro, and Premier — each unlocking more of the app (custom shop shapes, cabinetry tools, saved layout limits, and more). See the full breakdown on the Plan Comparison page.

Heads up: plan selection today is a local preview — it changes which features are unlocked in your browser, but no payment is collected yet. Real billing is coming; see our Terms of Service for the current status.

Shop Type: Woodworking, Mechanical, or Both

Kerph isn't just for woodshops. When you start a new layout in the Workshop Layout Planner (+ New Shop Layout or Edit Shop Settings), you'll see a Shop Type toggle at the top of the modal — it controls which tool categories show up in the sidebar catalog.

Woodworking
Table saws, bandsaws, jointers/planers, sanding, routing, turning, CNC, dust collection, and more.
Mechanical
Vehicle lifts, welding, air compressors, jacks & lifting equipment, tire changers, Snap-on/Mac Tools storage.
Both
Shows every category from both sets at once — for a combined wood shop & garage.

This choice is saved with the layout itself, not your account — so a "Workshop" layout and a "Garage" layout can each remember their own Shop Type. Categories that make sense for any shop (My Tools, Small Power Tools, Tool Storage, Lighting, Plumbing) always show, no matter which option you pick.

Tip: you can change Shop Type at any time from Edit Shop Settings — nothing already placed on your grid gets removed, it just changes what's available to drag in next.

Layers: Tools, Dust Collection, Lighting & Plumbing

Every item you place belongs to one of four layers. The floating Layers panel lets you show everything at once, or isolate just one layer to reduce visual clutter while you focus on a specific system.

Tools
Machines, cabinets, wall features, storage — the physical shop floor plan.
Dust Collection
Collectors, ductwork, fittings, and blast gates.
Lighting
Shop lights, high-bay fixtures, task lights.
Plumbing
Floor drains, utility sinks, water heaters, trench drains, hose bibs — works for any shop type.

Cabinets and wall features (doors, windows, outlets, interior walls) always stay visible regardless of which layer you're viewing — they're structural context for the room itself, not something you'd ever want to hide.

Workshop Layout Planner

Getting Started

  1. Click + New Shop Layout and set your shop's width, length, ceiling height, grid scale, and Shop Type. Pick a starter template if you want a head start, or start blank.
  2. Drag tools from the left sidebar catalog onto the grid. Hover any tool for a quick popover with its real footprint, power, voltage, and weight — plus a "Check current price" link where available.
  3. Click a placed item to select it; drag to reposition, use the rotation handle to turn it, or press Delete to remove it.
  4. Mark tools you actually own with the checkmark button, or star them as a wishlist item — both show up in My Tools on the home page and in this sidebar.

Floating panels

Cabinet Builder, Wall Features, Layers, Electrical Load, Clearance Checker, and Cost Rollup all live in small floating panels on the right side of the grid. Click a panel's title bar to expand/collapse it, or drag the title bar to move it out of the way.

Tip: use Undo (or Ctrl+Z) liberally — it's wired into every placement, drag, resize, and delete, so it's the fastest way to back out of a mistake.

Walls & Wall Features

The Wall Features panel lets you place doors, windows, switches, outlets, electrical panels, mini-splits, and interior walls directly onto your shop's boundary or interior.

  1. Open the Wall Features panel and pick a feature type.
  2. Click anywhere along an exterior wall (or draw an interior wall first, under Interior Wall) to place it.
  3. Drag a placed feature along its wall to reposition it, or use its edge handles to resize doors and windows.
  4. Outlets prompt for a mounting height and voltage (110V/220V) when placed — useful context once you get to the Electrical Load panel.
Tip: the Clearance Checker panel scans your layout for walkways narrower than a set minimum and flags tight spots in red — run it after you've placed your major tools to catch layout problems early.

Saving & Loading Layouts

Your current layout autosaves continuously to your account as you work — you'll never lose progress just by navigating away or closing the tab. Saved Layouts is a separate, named-snapshot system for keeping multiple versions.

  1. Click Save in the header, give the layout a name, and choose whether to save as a new layout or overwrite your most recent one.
  2. Open your account button (top-right) to see, load, or delete any saved layout.
  3. Saved layouts sync to your account, so they're available on any device you sign into.
Dust Collection

Magnetic Port Snapping

Every duct piece, elbow, Y-joint, T-joint, reducer, and blast gate has invisible "ports" at the points where it's meant to connect to another piece. When you drag one of these near a compatible open port, it automatically snaps into place — sliding and rotating itself so the two pieces line up flush and facing each other. You don't have to nudge things pixel-by-pixel to get a clean-looking run.

How to connect two pieces

  1. Place your first piece (usually starting from the dust collector) anywhere on the grid.
  2. Drag a second piece — a duct segment, elbow, Y-joint, T-joint, reducer, or blast gate — near one end of the first piece.
  3. You don't need to be exact. Once you're reasonably close to the open end, the piece snaps: it repositions and rotates itself so its port lines up exactly with the other piece's port.
  4. Keep going, piece by piece, working outward from the collector toward each tool.
Only matching duct sizes snap to each other. A 4" piece will only snap to another 4" piece, and a 6" piece only to another 6". The one exception is the Duct Reducer — see below. If a piece won't snap the way you expect, double-check both pieces are the same diameter — that's the most common reason.

How many ports does each piece have?

Ports are the connection points a piece is trying to snap. Some pieces only have two (a straight through-connection); fittings that split the airflow have three.

Straight Duct
2 ports (both ends)
Blast Gate
2 ports (both ends)
90° Elbow
2 ports (each leg)
Y-Joint
3 ports
T-Joint
3 ports

Blast gates and "open" ends

A blast gate is a valve, not a plain pass-through — in a real shop, one side connects back to the main trunk and the other side sits right at the tool it serves. That tool-facing side is supposed to end there; it isn't meant to connect to anything else, since tools themselves aren't part of the duct network in this planner.

That's why Check Duct Connections (in the Sizing Calculator panel) only flags a blast gate if it isn't hooked into the duct network on either side. A gate with one side properly connected and the other terminating at a tool is exactly right and won't be flagged.

Duct Drops (going from overhead down to a tool)

This grid is a top-down 2D view, so it can't show a pipe actually descending from an overhead trunk to a tool at floor height — every duct piece just floats at the same fixed mount elevation. A Duct Drop is how a vertical run is represented instead: place one where the overhead branch reaches a tool (typically right behind the blast gate), and it snaps into the run exactly like a duct segment does.

In 2D it shows as a plain bullseye icon rather than a pipe shape, since "direction" doesn't mean anything for a vertical connector in a top-down view. It counts as an elbow-equivalent in the sizing calculator (same friction-loss penalty, just for a change in elevation instead of heading), and it's the one dust-collection piece that renders as a real vertical pipe in the 3D Shop Viewer.

Tip: Placing a tool with Attach to dust collection? checked (in the Workshop Layout Planner catalog) automatically builds the blast gate, drop, and duct run for you — drops are inserted automatically, you don't have to add them by hand unless you're routing manually.

Verifying and using your run

  • 🔍 Check Duct Connections — scans every placed piece and marks any genuinely open (unconnected) duct end in red on the grid, so you can spot a gap in the run at a glance.
  • ⚡ Auto-Fill Branches from Layout — traces the connected run from your dust collector out to each blast gate and fills in the branch length and elbow count for you in the sizing calculator below, instead of measuring by hand.
Tip: Build outward from the collector one connected piece at a time rather than placing everything first and connecting it after — it's much easier to see exactly what's snapping to what as you go.

Reducers & Mixing 4"/6" Duct

Real shops often run a 6" main trunk off the collector for airflow, then step down to 4" branches near smaller tools. Kerph's 4"–6" Duct Reducer is built exactly for that transition — it's the one piece in the catalog with two different-sized ports on the same fitting.

6" main
Reducer
4" branch

Drag a Duct Reducer from the Dust Collection category (it sits above the 4"/6" subgroups, since it belongs to both) to the point where your run needs to change size. Its wide end snaps onto 6" pieces; its narrow end snaps onto 4" pieces — nothing else in the catalog will connect two different diameters together.

4" and 6" duct can both exist in the same layout freely — you're never restricted to one size per shop. The reducer is only needed at the specific point where a run actually changes diameter; everywhere else, just keep using matching sizes as usual.

Sizing Calculator

The Sizing Calculator (in the floating panel on the Dust Collection page) estimates whether your ductwork and collector can move enough air for the tools you're running.

  1. Pick your dust collector model from the dropdown, or enter its rated CFM manually.
  2. For each branch, either fill in duct length and elbow count by hand, or click Auto-Fill Branches from Layout to trace it from your actual drawn duct run.
  3. The calculator applies real friction-loss penalties per elbow/fitting and compares the result against your collector's fan curve at that static pressure — not just a flat CFM number.
  4. A rough material cost estimate totals up based on the duct, fittings, and collector you've placed.
Tip: if a branch is coming up short on CFM, try shortening the run, reducing elbow count, or switching that branch to a larger duct size (with a reducer where it meets the main trunk) before assuming you need a bigger collector.
Project Designer

Panels, Hardware & Cut List Handoff

  1. Click + Add Panel to define a piece — name, material, thickness, width, height, and quantity.
  2. Add hardware (hinges, slides, fasteners) to the Hardware Checklist as your project calls for it.
  3. Use the Notes field for anything you don't want to forget — it autosaves as you type.
  4. When your panel list is ready, click Send to Cut List Optimizer to hand every panel off for sheet/board layout — no need to re-enter dimensions.
Tip: duplicate a panel (rather than re-entering it) when you need several nearly-identical pieces — then just tweak the one dimension that's different.

3D Preview, Labels & Measurements

The 3D Preview tab renders your panel list as an actual 3D scene, colored by material, with a quantity badge on repeated pieces.

  • Select & drag a panel to reposition it in 3D; grid-snapping keeps things aligned. Use the free-rotate gizmo for angled assemblies.
  • Auto-Align flattens, flushes, or levels a selected piece against its neighbors in one click.
  • Labels let you tag specific pieces right on the 3D view — handy for noting orientation or a cut note before you head to the shop.
  • Measurements place a persistent on-diagram dimension between two points, which stays put as you keep working.
  • Undo works in the 3D view the same way it does everywhere else in Kerph.

Saved Projects

Your current project autosaves continuously, just like the Workshop Layout Planner. Use Save Project to keep a named snapshot you can return to, and the account button's project picker to switch between saved projects. Free accounts can have unlimited live projects but zero saved snapshots; Pro unlocks up to 5 saved projects, and Premier is unlimited — see Plan Comparison for current limits.

Cut List Optimizer

Sheets vs. Boards

The optimizer works in two stock modes, and picking the right one matters for getting a useful layout.

Sheet mode

For sheet goods (plywood, MDF, melamine) — set your sheet's width and height, and the optimizer packs your parts across the sheet's full 2D area, rotating pieces as needed to minimize waste.

Board mode

For dimensional lumber — set your board's length, and the optimizer arranges cuts end-to-end along its length only, accounting for kerf width between cuts.

  1. Add parts manually, or arrive here via Send to Cut List Optimizer from Project Designer.
  2. Set your stock size and kerf width in Settings.
  3. The optimizer lays out the most efficient cut diagram it can and reports total waste.
  4. Export the finished diagram to PDF to bring into the shop.
3D Shop Viewer

Orbiting & Editing in 3D

The 3D Shop Viewer renders your actual Workshop Layout Planner grid — tools, cabinets, wall features, dust collection, and lighting — as a real 3D scene, using the same layout data you've already built.

  • Orbit: click and drag to rotate the camera around your shop.
  • Zoom: scroll to move in and out.
  • Select & edit: click any placed item to select it, then drag to move, use the rotation handle to turn it, or press Delete to remove it — changes here sync back to your 2D layout.
  • Tooltips: hover an item for a quick info card, same as the 2D sidebar popover.
  • Duct Drops: the one dust-collection piece that actually renders as a vertical pipe here, since 3D can finally show the elevation change a 2D top-down view can't.
Find a Builder

Generating a Quote Request

Find a Builder turns your current shop layout into a written summary a contractor or dust-collection installer can actually quote from, instead of you re-describing your shop from scratch.

  1. Build your layout in the Workshop Layout Planner or Dust Collection page first — Find a Builder reads whatever your current shop looks like.
  2. Open Find a Builder and review the auto-generated summary (dimensions, placed tools, duct runs).
  3. Add any extra notes specific to your project.
  4. Generate a PDF of your shop layout to attach, then use the pre-filled email draft to send your request — note that email links can't attach files automatically, so you'll need to attach the downloaded PDF yourself before sending.
Community

Shop Showcase

Shop Showcase is a public gallery where you can post photos of your actual shop for inspiration and feedback from other Kerph users.

  • Click + Share Your Shop, add a photo, title, description, and tags, and post — you'll need to be signed in.
  • Browse, search, and sort posts by newest, most liked, or most discussed.
  • Click any post to view it full-size, like it, or leave a comment.
  • You can delete your own posts at any time from the post's detail view.
Posts and photos here are public — visible to every Kerph user, not just people you know. Don't post anything you wouldn't want broadly visible.

Cool Tool Reviews

Real, first-hand reviews from other woodworkers and shop owners — searchable by tool name and filterable by category.

  • Click + Write a Review, pick a category, rate 1–5 stars, and write your take — you'll need to be signed in.
  • Mark other users' reviews as Helpful to surface the most useful ones; sort by newest, highest rated, or most helpful.
  • Your posted name on a review is snapshotted at the time you post it, so a later username change won't rewrite old reviews.
